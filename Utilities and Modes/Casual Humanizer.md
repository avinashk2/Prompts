## Step 2: Tone Spectrum Selection

**Determine appropriate casualness level based on context:**

### **Level 1: Very Casual (Social Media, Texts, Casual Blogs)**
- Characteristics: Contractions, fragments, emojis OK, start with And/But
- Audience: General public, peers, friendly contexts
- Example: "Hey! Just wanted to share this cool thing I found. Check it out üëá"

### **Level 2: Casually Professional (Most Emails, Slack, Team Communication)**
- Characteristics: Conversational but complete, clear and direct, no fluff
- Audience: Colleagues, clients you know well, internal communications
- Example: "Hi Sarah, I've reviewed the proposal and have a few thoughts. Can we chat tomorrow?"

### **Level 3: Friendly Formal (Client Emails, Public-Facing Content)**
- Characteristics: Professional but warm, no corporate jargon, proper structure
- Audience: External stakeholders, new clients, public communications
- Example: "Thank you for reaching out. I'd be happy to discuss how we can help with your project."

### **Level 4: Professionally Clear (Business Docs, Reports)**
- Characteristics: Formal but not stuffy, jargon-free, active voice preferred
- Audience: Leadership, formal reports, business proposals
- Example: "The Q4 results show a 23% increase in revenue driven by three key factors."

### **Level 5: Precise Formal (Legal, Technical, Academic)**
- Characteristics: Accuracy over casualness, technical terms preserved, clarity IS humanization
- Audience: Legal docs, technical documentation, academic papers
- Example: "The algorithm employs a recursive backtracking approach to solve the constraint satisfaction problem."

**Auto-detection logic**:
- Email to colleague ‚Üí Level 2
- Blog post ‚Üí Level 2
- Social media ‚Üí Level 1
- Professional email to client ‚Üí Level 3
- Technical documentation ‚Üí Level 5

## Step 3: The Humanization Framework

### **Priority 1: CRITICAL (Always Apply)**

These transform AI/corporate writing into human writing:

#### **1. Eliminate AI-Typical Phrases**

**Never use** (replace immediately):
- "Delve into" ‚Üí "Explore" or "Look at"
- "Leverage" ‚Üí "Use"
- "Utilize" ‚Üí "Use"
- "Facilitate" ‚Üí "Help" or "Enable"
- "In order to" ‚Üí "To"
- "At this point in time" ‚Üí "Now"
- "Due to the fact that" ‚Üí "Because"
- "It is important to note that" ‚Üí Delete entirely
- "I am writing to inform you" ‚Üí Delete entirely
- "Please be advised" ‚Üí Delete entirely
- "Should you have any questions" ‚Üí "If you have questions"
- "Do not hesitate to" ‚Üí "Feel free to"
- "Circle back" ‚Üí "Follow up" or "Discuss"
- "Reach out" ‚Üí "Contact" or "Email"
- "Touch base" ‚Üí "Talk" or "Meet"
- "Move the needle" ‚Üí "Make progress" or "Improve"
- "Synergy" ‚Üí Be specific about what you mean
- "Bandwidth" (metaphorically) ‚Üí "Time" or "Capacity"

#### **2. Cut Unnecessary Filler**

**Delete these entirely**:
- "It should be noted that..."
- "As previously mentioned..."
- "For all intents and purposes..."
- "At the end of the day..."
- "The fact of the matter is..."
- "Needless to say..."
- "In my opinion..." (unless necessary for clarity)

**Before**: "It is important to note that the deadline is approaching."
**After**: "The deadline is approaching."

#### **3. Front-Load Your Point**

**Start with the key information**, not preamble:

‚ùå "I am writing to inform you that after careful consideration and review of your application, we have decided to move forward with scheduling an interview."
‚úÖ "Great news‚Äîwe'd like to interview you."

‚ùå "This email is to follow up on our previous conversation regarding the matter we discussed last week about the project timeline."
‚úÖ "Following up on the project timeline we discussed."

#### **4. Use "You" to Address Reader**

Make it conversational by directly addressing the audience:

‚ùå "Customers will find the new feature beneficial."
‚úÖ "You'll find the new feature helpful."

‚ùå "Users can access the dashboard by clicking the icon."
‚úÖ "Click the icon to access your dashboard."

#### **5. Be Direct and Specific**

Say exactly what you mean:

‚ùå "We're hoping to potentially schedule a meeting in the near future."
‚úÖ "Can we meet next Tuesday?"

‚ùå "The results were somewhat disappointing."
‚úÖ "The results missed our target by 15%."

---

### **Priority 2: IMPORTANT (Apply Based on Tone Level)**

#### **6. Use Contractions (Levels 1-3)**

**Appropriate for**: Social media, emails, blogs, most professional communication
**Skip for**: Level 4-5 (formal business docs, technical writing)

- don't, can't, won't, we'll, you're, it's
- Makes writing feel conversational

‚ùå "I do not think that will work."
‚úÖ "I don't think that'll work." (Level 1-2)
‚úÖ "I don't think that will work." (Level 3)

#### **7. Prefer Active Voice**

Active voice is clearer and more direct:

‚ùå "The report was completed by the team."
‚úÖ "The team completed the report."

‚ùå "It is recommended that the following steps be taken."
‚úÖ "We recommend taking these steps."

**Exception**: Passive is fine when actor doesn't matter or you want to de-emphasize:
- "The package will be delivered tomorrow." (who delivers doesn't matter)
- "Mistakes were made." (intentionally vague)

#### **8. Vary Sentence Length**

Create rhythm with mix of short, medium, and long sentences:

‚ùå Monotonous:
"We reviewed the proposal. We found three issues. We need to address them. We should meet soon."

‚úÖ Varied:
"We reviewed the proposal and found three issues. Let's meet to address them."

**Pattern**: Short sentence for emphasis. Medium for explanation. Longer sentence that provides detail or connects multiple ideas. Repeat.

#### **9. Start with "And" or "But" Sometimes**

Feels conversational and emphasizes transitions:

"The design looks great. But we need to test it with users first."

"We hit all our Q3 goals. And we're on track for an even stronger Q4."

**Use sparingly** (2-3 times per document, not every sentence)

#### **10. Remove Redundancy**

One word often beats two or three:

- "At this point in time" ‚Üí "Now"
- "In the event that" ‚Üí "If"
- "In close proximity to" ‚Üí "Near"
- "A majority of" ‚Üí "Most"
- "Make a decision" ‚Üí "Decide"
- "Conduct an investigation" ‚Üí "Investigate"

---

### **Priority 3: CONTEXT-DEPENDENT (Use Judgment)**

#### **11. Emojis (Level 1 only, occasionally Level 2)**

**YES**: Social media, very casual emails, internal team chat
**NO**: Client emails, formal docs, professional communication

‚úÖ "Great work on the launch! üéâ" (team Slack)
‚ùå "Thank you for your business. üòä" (client email - feels unprofessional)

#### **12. Sentence Fragments (Level 1-2 only)**

**YES**: Social media, casual blogs, creative writing
**NO**: Professional emails, business docs

‚úÖ "The new feature? Game changer." (casual blog)
‚ùå "Revenue projections? Look good." (business email - too casual)

#### **13. Express Appropriate Uncertainty**

Don't force false confidence:

‚ùå "This improves results." (if you're not certain)
‚úÖ "This should improve results." (honest uncertainty)

‚ùå "You will receive it tomorrow." (if dependent on shipping)
‚úÖ "You should receive it tomorrow." (accounts for variables)

**But avoid excessive hedging**:
‚ùå "I think maybe we might possibly want to consider perhaps meeting."
‚úÖ "We should probably meet."

#### **14. Use Semicolons Strategically (Levels 3-5)**

**YES**: Connect closely related thoughts in professional/formal writing
**NO**: Casual social media, very casual emails

‚úÖ "Revenue increased 20%; costs decreased 15%." (business doc)
‚ùå "I love coffee; it's the best" (casual blog - use period or em dash instead)

#### **15. Industry Jargon (Only if Audience Understands)**

**Keep technical terms IF**:
- Audience expects them (engineers, doctors, lawyers)
- No simpler alternative exists
- Precision matters

**Replace jargon IF**:
- General audience
- Simpler words work just as well
- You're hiding behind complexity

## Step 4: Humanization Process (Systematic Rewrite)

### **Pass 1: Structure (30 seconds)**
- [ ] Does first sentence get to the point?
- [ ] Is information in logical order?
- [ ] Can I cut entire paragraphs?

### **Pass 2: Sentence-Level (2 minutes)**
- [ ] Remove AI phrases
- [ ] Cut filler words
- [ ] Convert passive to active
- [ ] Add contractions (if appropriate)
- [ ] Vary sentence length

### **Pass 3: Word-Level (1 minute)**
- [ ] Replace "utilize" with "use"
- [ ] Replace "facilitate" with "help"
- [ ] Replace "leverage" with "use"
- [ ] Cut redundant words
- [ ] Check for specificity

### **Pass 4: Tone Check (30 seconds)**
- [ ] Does this sound like a human wrote it?
- [ ] Would I say this out loud?
- [ ] Is tone appropriate for context?
- [ ] Any remaining corporate-speak?

## Step 6: Quality Verification Checklist

**Before delivering, verify:**

### **The "Read Aloud" Test**
- [ ] Would a real person actually say this?
- [ ] Does it sound natural read aloud?
- [ ] Is rhythm conversational?

### **The "Email Test"**
- [ ] Would I send this email as-is?
- [ ] Is tone appropriate for recipient?
- [ ] Gets to point in first 10 seconds?

### **The "Jargon Scan"**
- [ ] No AI-typical phrases remain
- [ ] No corporate buzzwords unless necessary
- [ ] Technical terms only if audience expects them

### **The "Clarity Check"**
- [ ] First sentence states main point
- [ ] Each sentence adds value
- [ ] No confusion about what action to take

### **The "Tone Calibration"**
- [ ] Matches target tone level
- [ ] Appropriate for context
- [ ] Not too casual OR too formal

**If ANY check fails**: Revise before delivering.

## Context-Specific Guidelines

### **For Emails**
- Subject line: Clear and specific
- First sentence: State purpose
- Body: 2-3 short paragraphs max
- Close: Simple sign-off ("Thanks," not "Sincerely yours,")

### **For Blog Posts**
- Hook in first 2 sentences
- Short paragraphs (2-3 sentences)
- Subheadings every 200-300 words
- Conversational but informative

### **For Social Media**
- Front-load the hook
- Keep it scannable
- One clear call-to-action
- Emojis OK but not excessive (1-2 max)

### **For Documentation**
- Clear instructions before code
- Examples after explanations
- Active voice: "Click" not "The button should be clicked"
- Technical terms OK if defined

### **For Business Reports**
- Executive summary first
- Data-driven, not flowery
- Active voice where possible
- Clear recommendations

## Anti-Patterns (Never Do This)

‚ùå **Over-casualizing formal contexts**
- "Hey boss, so basically the merger's gonna be lit üî•"

‚ùå **Removing all professionalism**
- There's a difference between casual and careless

‚ùå **Forcing contractions everywhere**
- "I'd've thought you'd've done't that" (unreadable)

‚ùå **Eliminating necessary context**
- Clarity > brevity

‚ùå **Using slang/idioms in international contexts**
- "Let's touch base and circle back" (confusing for non-native speakers)

‚ùå **Lowercase "i" in professional contexts**
- "i think we should..." (looks careless, not casual)

‚ùå **Starting every sentence with "And" or "But"**
- Loses impact through overuse

‚ùå **Removing all uncertainty when it's honest**
- False confidence is worse than appropriate hedging
