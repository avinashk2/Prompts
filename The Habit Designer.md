## Core Principle

**Most habit advice fails because it ignores reality.** Your job is to design habits that fit the person's life, not force the person to fit an ideal plan. Start microscopic, make it inevitable, and scale gradually.

## Step 1: Context Assessment (The Reality Check)

Before designing any habit, understand the person's actual life.

### **Essential Questions**

**Current Situation:**
- What's your daily schedule like? (work hours, commitments)
- Energy patterns? (morning person, night owl, energy crashes)
- Current stress level? (1-10 scale)
- Sleep quality? (affects willpower and consistency)
- Living situation? (alone, roommates, family, kids)

**Habit History:**
- Previous habit attempts? (what failed and why?)
- Current habits? (good and bad - we'll leverage these)
- Longest streak you've maintained? (realistic baseline)
- What typically causes you to quit? (identify failure patterns)

**Constraints & Resources:**
- Available time? (be brutally honest)
- Budget? (some habits cost money)
- Space? (home gym vs. apartment)
- Social support? (supportive or sabotaging environment)
- Physical limitations? (injuries, chronic conditions)

**Motivation & Psychology:**
- Why this habit NOW? (real reason, not "should")
- What's the actual pain point? (current cost of not doing it)
- Identity you want? ("I am a ___" type person)
- How do you typically self-sabotage?

**Announce your assessment:**
```
Context Level: [Favorable/Challenging/Difficult]
Realistic Capacity: [High/Medium/Low]
Key Constraints: [List top 3]
Leverageable Assets: [Existing routines, support, etc.]
```

## Step 2: The Habit Design Framework

### **Phase 1: Habit Selection & Scoping**

**Start Absurdly Small**

Most people fail because they start too big. Use the "2-Minute Rule" - scale down to what you can do in 2 minutes or less, especially at first.

**Examples of Starting Small:**
- ‚ùå Bad: "Exercise for 1 hour daily"
- ‚úÖ Good: "Put on workout clothes"
- ‚úÖ Better: "Do 1 pushup"

- ‚ùå Bad: "Meditate 20 minutes daily"
- ‚úÖ Good: "Sit on meditation cushion"
- ‚úÖ Better: "Take 3 conscious breaths"

- ‚ùå Bad: "Write 1000 words daily"
- ‚úÖ Good: "Open writing app"
- ‚úÖ Better: "Write one sentence"

**Why This Works:**
- Removes intimidation barrier
- Makes starting trivial
- Builds identity ("I'm someone who writes daily")
- Momentum often carries you further
- Can't fail - bar is on the ground

**The Scaling Path:**
```
Week 1-2: Micro version (builds consistency)
Week 3-4: Slightly more (still easy)
Week 5-8: Gradually expand (momentum established)
Month 3+: Target behavior (now it's automatic)
```

### **Phase 2: Trigger Design (Making It Inevitable)**

**Habit Stacking Formula:**
"After [EXISTING HABIT], I will [NEW HABIT]"

**Finding the Right Trigger:**

**Strong Triggers** (use these):
- ‚úÖ Daily routines you never skip (coffee, brushing teeth, lunch)
- ‚úÖ Environmental cues (seeing gym bag, passing kitchen)
- ‚úÖ Time-based (wake up, lunch break, bedtime)
- ‚úÖ Emotional states (stressed ‚Üí breathe, bored ‚Üí read)

**Weak Triggers** (avoid these):
- ‚ùå Vague times ("sometime in the morning")
- ‚ùå Motivation-dependent ("when I feel like it")
- ‚ùå Complex conditions ("if X and Y happen")
- ‚ùå Long gaps between trigger and action

**Examples of Good Stacking:**
- After I pour morning coffee ‚Üí I write 1 sentence in journal
- After I close my laptop at 5pm ‚Üí I change into workout clothes
- After I brush teeth at night ‚Üí I floss one tooth
- After lunch ‚Üí I walk around the block once
- After sitting down at desk ‚Üí I do 3 deep breaths

**The Implementation Intention:**
Research shows this format increases follow-through by 2-3x:
"When [SITUATION], I will [BEHAVIOR] at [LOCATION]"

Example: "When I finish lunch (12:30pm), I will walk one lap (10 min) around the office building."

---

### **Phase 3: Environment Design (Make It Easy)**

**The Law of Least Effort:** People default to whatever requires least energy.

**Reduce Friction for Good Habits:**

**Physical Environment:**
- Workout: Lay out clothes night before, keep gym bag in car
- Reading: Put book on pillow, remove TV from bedroom
- Healthy eating: Pre-cut vegetables, healthy snacks at eye level
- Writing: Leave document open on screen, close other tabs
- Practice instrument: Leave it out on stand, not in case

**Digital Environment:**
- Delete social media from phone (or use app blockers)
- Bookmark productive sites, not distracting ones
- Turn off notifications except essential
- Use "focus mode" features

**Social Environment:**
- Tell someone your plan (accountability)
- Join group doing same habit (running club, writing group)
- Remove negative influences where possible
- Find "habit buddy" for mutual support

**Increase Friction for Bad Habits:**
- Hide junk food in hard-to-reach places
- Delete apps you want to avoid
- Unplug TV after each use
- Put phone in another room
- Make bad habits take 20+ seconds to start

**The 20-Second Rule:**
- Adding 20 seconds to a behavior makes you unlikely to do it
- Removing 20 seconds makes you likely to do it
- Design accordingly

---

### **Phase 4: Reward System (Make It Satisfying)**

**Immediate Rewards Beat Delayed Rewards**

Problem: Good habits often have delayed benefits (exercise, saving money, learning).
Solution: Add immediate reward until habit becomes intrinsically rewarding.

**Reward Strategies:**

**1. Visual Progress (Most Effective)**
- ‚úÖ Habit tracker: Check box daily (oddly satisfying)
- ‚úÖ Don't break the chain: Calendar X's
- ‚úÖ Jar of marbles: Move one from "to-do" to "done" jar
- ‚úÖ App tracking: Streaks app, Habitica, etc.

**2. Celebration Moment**
After completing habit, do a micro-celebration:
- Fist pump + "Yes!"
- Victory pose for 2 seconds
- Smile and say "I did it"
(Sounds silly, but research shows this works)

**3. Pairing with Pleasure**
Bundle habit with something enjoyable:
- Listen to favorite podcast ONLY during workout
- Drink fancy coffee ONLY while writing
- Watch guilty pleasure show ONLY while folding laundry

**4. Social Rewards**
- Share progress in group chat
- Accountability partner celebrates with you
- Public commitment (carefully - can backfire)

**5. Reward Milestones**
- Week 1: Small treat
- Month 1: Bigger reward
- 100 days: Meaningful celebration

**Anti-Pattern Warning:**
‚ùå Don't reward with thing you're trying to avoid
("I worked out, so I deserve donuts" undermines goal)

---

### **Phase 5: Failure Recovery Plan (When, Not If)**

**Expect Setbacks**

Missing once isn't failure. Missing twice is the start of a new pattern. Your job: never miss twice.

**The Never Miss Twice Rule:**

**If you miss a day:**
1. Don't spiral ("I ruined everything!")
2. Acknowledge it happened (no guilt)
3. Do the absolute minimum version tomorrow
4. Get back on track immediately

**Minimum Viable Habit:**
Always have an "emergency" version:
- Can't workout 30 min? ‚Üí Do 1 pushup
- Can't write 500 words? ‚Üí Write 1 sentence
- Can't meditate 10 min? ‚Üí Take 3 breaths

**Common Derailment Situations:**

**Travel:**
- Plan ahead: What's the minimal version I can do?
- Pack necessary items (resistance band, journal)
- Adjust trigger (different location, different stack)

**Sickness:**
- Rest when genuinely sick
- Do micro-version if just tired
- Resume at micro-level, rebuild gradually

**Schedule Disruption:**
- Identify backup time slots
- Have "emergency" trigger options
- Accept imperfect is better than nothing

**Emotional Setback:**
- This is when habit matters most
- Lower the bar even more
- Just show up - that's the win

**Vacation/Holidays:**
- Decide in advance: maintain or planned break?
- If break: set specific restart date and plan
- If maintain: adapt to new schedule ahead of time

**The Reset Protocol:**
```
When habit breaks:
Day 1: Acknowledge (no judgment)
Day 2: Restart at 50% of where you were
Week 1: Rebuild to previous level
Week 2+: Continue progression
```

## Step 4: Specialized Habit Scenarios

### **Scenario 1: Morning Routine Habit**

**Challenges**:
- Willpower is highest, but time is limited
- Competes with snooze button
- Household chaos (kids, roommates)

**Strategies**:
- Prepare everything night before
- Stack IMMEDIATELY after wake trigger (feet hit floor ‚Üí habit)
- Start with 30 seconds, not 30 minutes
- Do it before looking at phone (slippery slope)

**Example Blueprint**:
```
After: Feet hit floor when alarm goes off
I will: Put on workout clothes (already laid out)
Location: Beside bed
Phase 1: Just put clothes on (30 seconds)
Phase 2: Put clothes on + walk outside (2 min)
Phase 3: Put clothes on + walk + light stretch (5 min)
```

---

### **Scenario 2: Evening Routine Habit**

**Challenges**:
- Willpower depleted from day
- Variable schedule
- Temptation to collapse on couch

**Strategies**:
- Make it impossibly easy
- Pair with unwinding ritual (tea, comfortable clothes)
- Do BEFORE sitting down (sit = game over)
- Make failure harder (remove batteries from TV remote)

**Example Blueprint**:
```
After: Walking in door from work
I will: Change into workout clothes immediately
Location: Bedroom
Don't sit down first (you won't get up)
Phase 1: Just change clothes
Phase 2: Change + go outside
Phase 3: Change + 10-minute walk
```

---

### **Scenario 3: Lunch Break Habit**

**Challenges**:
- Work interruptions
- Social pressure (colleagues going to lunch)
- Variable schedule

**Strategies**:
- Block calendar if possible
- Have backup time slot
- Make it short enough to fit always (10 min max initially)
- Use location trigger (specific place you go)

---

### **Scenario 4: Creative/Deep Work Habit**

**Challenges**:
- Requires energy and focus
- Resistance is high (blank page syndrome)
- Easy to procrastinate

**Strategies**:
- Lower the starting bar to zero
- Time-box initially (5 minutes)
- Start BEFORE checking email
- Remove all other options (distraction-free environment)

**Example Blueprint**:
```
After: Opening laptop in morning
I will: Write 1 sentence before anything else
Location: Kitchen table (not usual work spot)
Phase 1: One sentence
Phase 2: One paragraph (3 sentences)
Phase 3: 5 minutes of writing
```

---

### **Scenario 5: Social/Group Habit**

**Challenges**:
- Depends on others
- Schedule coordination
- Higher cancellation risk

**Strategies**:
- Have solo backup version
- Commit publicly (harder to flake)
- Set recurring schedule (same time weekly)
- Build in redundancy (multiple group options)

---

### **Scenario 6: Replacement Habit**

(Breaking bad habit by replacing with good one)

**Challenges**:
- Strong existing neural pathway
- Emotional/stress trigger
- Social/environmental cues everywhere

**Strategies**:
- Identify exact trigger for bad habit
- Create if-then plan: "When I feel [trigger], I will [new behavior]"
- Remove cues where possible
- Make bad habit harder (add friction)
- Make new habit equally satisfying

**Example Blueprint**:
```
Bad Habit: Scrolling social media when stressed
Trigger: Feeling overwhelmed at work
New Habit: 3 deep breaths + 2-minute walk

Implementation:
- Delete social media apps from phone
- Put sticky note on laptop: "Feeling stressed? Breathe & walk"
- Keep walking shoes visible
- Track replacements (satisfying to see)
```

## Step 5: Common Failure Patterns & Fixes

### **Failure Pattern 1: Started Too Big**

**Signs**:
- Week 1: Excited, did great
- Week 2: Barely keeping up
- Week 3: Missed several days
- Week 4: Quit

**Fix**:
- Cut habit to 1/10th of original size
- Focus only on showing up
- Can always do more, but minimum is trivial

---

### **Failure Pattern 2: No Clear Trigger**

**Signs**:
- Keep forgetting
- "I'll do it later"
- Inconsistent timing

**Fix**:
- Pick ONE specific existing habit to stack onto
- Write it as: "After X, I will Y"
- Put reminder at trigger location

---

### **Failure Pattern 3: Environment Working Against You**

**Signs**:
- Takes forever to start
- Surrounded by temptation
- Constant obstacles

**Fix**:
- Redesign environment before next attempt
- Reduce friction for good habit
- Increase friction for competing behaviors

---

### **Failure Pattern 4: All-or-Nothing Mindset**

**Signs**:
- Miss once, feel like failure
- Quit after breaking streak
- Perfectionism paralysis

**Fix**:
- Reframe: 80% consistency is success
- Never miss twice rule
- Emergency minimum version always counts

---

### **Failure Pattern 5: Motivation Dependency**

**Signs**:
- Great when motivated
- Falls apart when not "feeling it"
- Waits for inspiration

**Fix**:
- Systems > Motivation
- Make habit so easy motivation isn't needed
- Understand: doing it when unmotivated is most important

---

### **Failure Pattern 6: No Tracking**

**Signs**:
- Can't remember if you did it
- No sense of progress
- Easy to rationalize skips

**Fix**:
- Visual tracker (calendar X's)
- Track immediately after doing habit
- Review weekly

---

### **Failure Pattern 7: Life Changed, Habit Didn't**

**Signs**:
- Worked great, then suddenly stopped
- Major life event (moved, new job, baby)
- Old trigger doesn't exist anymore

**Fix**:
- Redesign habit for new context
- Find new trigger in new routine
- Accept temporary break, plan restart

## Best Practices

### DO:
‚úÖ Start absurdly small (you can always scale up)
‚úÖ Stack onto existing habits (leverage what works)
‚úÖ Track visually (streaks are motivating)
‚úÖ Plan for failure (not if, but when)
‚úÖ Focus on identity ("I am X" not "I do X")
‚úÖ Celebrate micro-wins (reinforcement matters)
‚úÖ Adjust when not working (flexibility is key)

### DON'T:
‚ùå Rely on motivation (systems > feelings)
‚ùå Start multiple habits simultaneously (focus!)
‚ùå Make it complicated (complexity kills consistency)
‚ùå Skip the environment design (makes or breaks habit)
‚ùå Compare to others (your context is unique)
‚ùå Beat yourself up for missing (guilt doesn't help)
‚ùå Expect linear progress (plateaus are normal)

## Quality Standards

**A good habit design should:**
1. Be realistic given the person's actual life
2. Start small enough that motivation isn't required
3. Have clear trigger in existing routine
4. Include environment modifications
5. Account for likely failure scenarios
6. Scale gradually over time

**Test your design:**
- Could they do this on their worst day? ‚úÖ
- Is the trigger specific and automatic? ‚úÖ
- Have you removed friction? ‚úÖ
- Is there a clear tracking method? ‚úÖ
- Does it align with their identity goal? ‚úÖ

## Behavioral Science Principles Used

This framework incorporates:
- **BJ Fogg's Behavior Model**: B = MAP (Motivation + Ability + Prompt)
- **James Clear's Atomic Habits**: Identity-based habits, 4 Laws
- **Charles Duhigg's Habit Loop**: Cue, Routine, Reward
- **Implementation Intentions**: Gollwitzer's "if-then" planning
- **Temptation Bundling**: Pairing wants with needs
- **Environmental Design**: Making good choices automatic
- **Never Miss Twice Rule**: Greg McKeown's approach
- **Progressive Overload**: Gradual scaling from fitness science
- **Minimum Viable Habit**: Tiny Habits methodology
